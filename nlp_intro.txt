Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a field of artificial intelligence that focuses on enabling computers to understand, interpret, and generate human language. Human communication is complex because it involves grammar, context, meaning, and ambiguity. NLP attempts to bridge the gap between human language and computer understanding. Over the years, NLP has evolved from rule-based systems to machine learning and deep learning based approaches. Early NLP systems relied heavily on manually designed linguistic rules, but these systems struggled with the diversity and complexity of natural language. With the growth of computational power and the availability of large text datasets, statistical and machine learning approaches became dominant in NLP. Today, NLP techniques are widely used in applications such as chatbots, machine translation, sentiment analysis, question answering systems, and search engines.

One of the fundamental tasks in NLP is text preprocessing. Before any machine learning model can analyze text, the text must be cleaned and structured. Preprocessing includes steps such as tokenization, sentence segmentation, lowercasing, stopword removal, and stemming or lemmatization. Tokenization splits text into smaller units called tokens, usually words. Stopword removal eliminates common words such as "the", "is", and "and" which do not carry significant meaning for many NLP tasks. Stemming reduces words to their base forms, helping reduce vocabulary size. These preprocessing steps improve the efficiency of models and reduce noise in textual data.

Another important aspect of NLP is semantic understanding. Words can have multiple meanings depending on context. For example, the word "bank" can refer to a financial institution or the side of a river. NLP models attempt to resolve such ambiguities using context from surrounding words. Techniques such as word embeddings, language models, and contextual embeddings help capture semantic relationships between words. These techniques allow NLP systems to recognize that words like "car" and "automobile" have similar meanings even though they are different words. Understanding such relationships is essential for building semantic search engines, recommendation systems, and conversational AI systems.
